Mountains

Aoraki / Mount Cook

Aoraki/Mount Cook is New Zealand's highest mountain, located in the Southern Alps within Aoraki/Mount Cook National Park. The mountain holds significant cultural importance for the Māori people, who call it Aoraki, meaning "cloud piercer". It is a popular destination for mountaineering, hiking, and viewing glaciers, with a landscape defined by dramatic valleys, glaciers, and alpine peaks.

Mount Tongariro

Mount Tongariro is a complex, active volcano in New Zealand's North Island, located within the Tongariro National Park, New Zealand's first national park. It is known for its dramatic volcanic landscapes, including craters, emerald lakes, and lava flows, which have made it famous for features like the Tongariro Alpine Crossing and its use as a filming location for the Lord of the Rings trilogy. The area is also a dual UNESCO World Heritage site due to its outstanding natural features and significant cultural importance to the Māori people.

Te Papa Museum

Te Papa is New Zealand's national museum, located in Wellington, and is known for being interactive, innovative, and bicultural. The museum's name translates from Maori to "our container of treasured things" and it features collections on the country's natural environment, art, history, and unique Maori culture. It's a popular and accessible institution, with general admission being free for residents, and it houses extensive collections and offers dynamic exhibitions and events.

Auckland War Memorial Museum

The Auckland War Memorial Museum is a neoclassical museum in Auckland, New Zealand, that serves as both a memorial to those who fought for New Zealand and a major cultural institution. Opened in 1929, it contains significant collections on Maori and Pacific artifacts, New Zealand's social and military history, and natural history. It is a popular tourist attraction, though it is currently undergoing redevelopment, which may affect certain galleries.

City Highlights

City in New Zealand.

Auckland

Auckland is New Zealand's largest city, located on the North Island, and is a major center for business and commerce. Known as the "City of Sails," it's famous for its harbors, islands, and sailing culture, being situated between two harbors: the Waitemata Harbour and the Manukau Harbour. The city is multicultural, with a diverse population and significant Polynesian and Asian communities, and offers a mix of urban and natural attractions like beaches, volcanic cones, and islands.

Wellington

Wellington is New Zealand's capital city, located on the southern tip of the North Island where the city is nestled between a harbor and green hills. It is known as the country's cultural and political center, famous for its vibrant arts scene, many museums like Te Papa, and a lively, energetic atmosphere often associated with its famously windy weather. The city's compact layout allows for walkability and a blend of natural and urban attractions, including nearby Zealandia and a bustling waterfront.

Queenstown

Queenstown is known as the "Adventure Capital of the World." It's famous for extreme sports like bungee jumping, skiing, hiking, and scenic flights. The town offers breathtaking scenery and outdoor adventures all year round, making it one of the top travel destinations in New Zealand.
